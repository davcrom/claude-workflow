---
name: grant-write
description: Invoke when writing or editing any part of a grant proposal.
---

Ask upfront if not already known:
- Funder and mechanism?
- Stage (drafting, revising, responding to reviews)?
- Which section?

Write for a skeptical reviewer with limited time. Active voice. Specific over vague. Cut hedging that weakens the case without adding scientific precision. Avoid "we hope to", "we aim to explore" — use "we will determine / measure / test".

---

**Opening section** (Specific Aims, Project Summary, Synopsis, etc.)
- Hook: the problem and why it matters now
- Gap: what is missing in current knowledge
- Hypothesis: central, testable claim
- Aims: 2–4 concrete, feasible aims that do not all fail if one fails
- Be affirmative and specific; support claims with solid references
- If the opening oversells relative to budget/duration, reviewers will penalise it

**State of the art / Introduction**
- First paragraph: broad and accessible — introduce the phenomenon in plain terms, state the core problem intuitively, gesture at the solution. No technical detail, notation, or parameters until the reader is oriented.
- Body: include the team's own prior work, competing methodologies, and an explicit innovation claim
- Critical synthesis — do not list publications; explain what is missing and why your approach addresses the gap
- Make clear how ambitious the objectives are and how they go beyond current state of the art

**Significance:** frame around impact, not just interest

**Innovation:** be specific about what is new — not just "novel"

**Research plan / Approach**
The overview covers strategy and prediction; detailed methods belong in task descriptions, not here.

Ensure the overview addresses:
1. What is the problem, what challenges does it pose, and what is the "big idea" to overcome it?
2. Why is the problem important? (State this explicitly — do not assume reviewers will infer it.)
3. What methodologies will be adopted, and why are they suitable?
4. What are the expected results?
5. What contingency plans address potential bottlenecks? (At least one per aim/task.)
6. What is the role of each team member, and how will they collaborate?

If the overview exceeds its character/page limit, the most common cause is task-level detail (software names, equipment, analysis pipelines) that belongs in task descriptions — move it there.

**Task / Aim descriptions**
Each task description should cover:
- Objectives in the context of the project
- Methodologies
- Expected results and how they are preconditions for subsequent tasks
- Role of different participants
- Justification of resources assigned to the task
- Contingency: if the primary approach fails, name the fallback explicitly

**Citing evidence accurately**
- Before citing any study, identify the proposal's central claim precisely
- Ask: does this study directly test this claim, or is it tangentially related?
- Give full credit to prior work for what it actually shows — do not undersell it
- Do not present tangential findings as definitive evidence; flag what remains untested
- Do not attribute the project's novel contribution to existing literature — frame unestablished claims as a gap, not a finding

**Budget**
- All figures must be independently justifiable (supplier quotes, confirmed rates, explicit PI input). Never reverse-engineer to hit a ceiling.
- Flag unused headroom with real options — do not silently fill with invented line items.
- Use `≈€` / `≈$` for approximate amounts, not `~` (renders as strikethrough in some terminals).

---

**Funder-specific notes**

*NIH:* Specific Aims page is one page and carries disproportionate weight — reviewers often score based on it alone. Innovation and Approach are scored criteria.

*NSF:* Broader Impacts is a scored criterion, not an afterthought — address it substantively.

*ERC:* Emphasise the PI's vision and intellectual leadership; proposals are evaluated as much on the researcher as the project.
