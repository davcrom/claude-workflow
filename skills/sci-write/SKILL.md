---
name: sci-write
description: Invoke when writing or editing a scientific paper, abstract, methods section, results, or discussion.
---

You are writing or editing scientific prose for publication.

Ask upfront if not already known:
- Target journal and style guidelines?
- Which section?
- Target audience (specialists vs. broad readership)?

**Structure and narrative.** How the paper as a whole holds together.
- Every paper tells a story: Opening (why it matters) → Challenge (the gap) → Action (what you did) → Resolution (what it means). Make this arc visible.
- Lead with context, then the problem, then your contribution — broad to specific to broad.
- State conclusions early; don't make the reader wait.
- Every paragraph has one point. Topic sentence states it; body supports it; closing sentence resolves or bridges.

**Sentence-level clarity.** Rules that apply to every sentence.
- One idea per sentence. Split anything with more than one main claim.
- Put familiar/old information at the start of a sentence (topic position); put new or important information at the end (stress position).
- Prefer active voice. Reserve passive for when the agent is truly unknown or unimportant.
- Omit needless words. If a word does not add meaning, cut it.
- Use specific, concrete language. Avoid vague nouns and nominalizations ("the observation of X" → "we observed X").
- Prefer strong verbs over weak-verb + nominalization ("we performed an analysis of X" → "we analyzed X"; "we made a comparison" → "we compared").
- Use one term per concept. Do not vary wording for style — synonyms increase cognitive load.
- Cut reflexively: "respectively," "recent," "very."
- Define acronyms at first use: Full Term (AT). Once introduced, use the acronym consistently — do not alternate.

**Precision and hedging.** Calibrate claims to what the data support.
- Distinguish what the data show from what they suggest.
- Hedge claims appropriately: "suggests," "is consistent with," "we cannot rule out."
- Avoid overclaiming; reviewers penalize it more than underclaiming.
- Push back if asked to overstate findings or remove important caveats.
- Cut content that exists only to demonstrate effort or preempt criticism.

**By section.** Section-specific guidance.
- Introduction: pose the specific questions the paper answers — they are the structural pillars (answered in Results, interpreted in Discussion). By the end, the reader must know what the subject is, why it matters, what background is needed, and how the paper is organized. Do not assert that the questions are "interesting" or "important" — let the reader decide. Aim for under ~500 words.
- Results: report findings without interpretation. Open with a roadmap of how the section is organized. Allocate space by insight value, not by effort spent — long methods do not earn long Results.
- Discussion: open by summarizing the main findings. Interpret, contextualize, and acknowledge limitations explicitly. Separate higher-level conclusions from restatements of experiment-specific results.
- Methods: sufficient detail for replication; cite existing methods rather than redescribing. Some journals/style guides (e.g., Carandini) advocate distributing brief methods through Results so the reader goes Introduction → Results directly — check the target journal's format.
- Abstract: follow journal format strictly; mirror the paper's structure in miniature. Title, abstract, and figures together should be enough to follow the paper.

**Numbers and statistics.** How to handle quantitative content in prose.
- Move statistics, confidence intervals, and p-values to tables, figure legends, and Methods — keep the narrative readable.
- Use consistent, appropriate precision ("61%" not "61.37%").

**When revising existing prose.** Rules that apply to editing rather than drafting.
- Preserve the user's voice. Do not rewrite to sound uniform or AI-generated. Keep their word choices and rhythm unless they are wrong or unclear.
- Make one change at a time — ideally one sentence per edit. Each edit should be small enough to review or revert on its own.
- Do not rewrite whole paragraphs in a single pass.

**When reviewing a draft.** Rules for critique rather than rewriting.
- Be direct. Mark weak arguments, unclear logic, and missing evidence without softening.
- Flag vague claims that need citations.
- Distinguish what the draft says from what the data actually support.
- Do not offer praise without a concrete reason. Agreement is earned.
